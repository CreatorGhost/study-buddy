(c) DNA replication between divisions

**Answer:** (c) DNA replication between divisions

### Q3.
How is apomixis beneficial to farmers?

**Answer:**
Apomixis allows seed production without fertilization, resulting in offspring that are genetically identical to the parent plant. This ensures:
1. **Uniformity** — Crops show consistent characteristics and yield, as there is no genetic variation.
2. **Cost-effectiveness** — Farmers can use seeds from hybrid crops year after year without purchasing new hybrid seeds each season, reducing costs.

### Q5.
Define genetic drift. Give an example.

**Answer:**
**Genetic drift** is the random change in allele frequencies in a population due to chance events (not due to natural selection), typically having a more pronounced effect in small populations.

**Example:** The **bottleneck effect** in cheetah populations — cheetahs went through a severe population bottleneck, reducing genetic diversity drastically. The surviving small population had limited allele variation, and random events determined which alleles persisted, exemplifying genetic drift.

Mention any two functions of placenta during pregnancy.

### Q7.
Explain how operons are regulated in prokaryotes.

**Answer:**
Operons in prokaryotes are units of gene regulation consisting of structural genes, a promoter, and an operator. They can be regulated in two ways:

1. **Inducible operons** (e.g., *lac* operon in *E. coli*): In the absence of a substrate (inducer), a repressor protein binds to the operator and blocks transcription. When the inducer (e.g., lactose) is present, it binds to the repressor, causing it to dissociate from the operator, thereby allowing RNA polymerase to transcribe the structural genes.

2. **Repressible operons:** The operon is normally active (ON), but when the end product (co-repressor) accumulates, it binds to the repressor activating it, which then binds the operator and switches transcription OFF.

This mechanism ensures that genes are expressed only when needed, conserving cellular energy and resources.

**Example:** **Hexaploid wheat** (*Triticum aestivum*) has six sets of chromosomes (6n = 42). It arose through natural polyploidization events involving hybridization between different grass species. Polyploidy often results in larger, more vigorous plants, which is agriculturally advantageous.

### Q11.
What are the major steps involved in PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)?

**Answer:**
The three major steps of PCR, carried out in repeated cycles, are:

1. **Denaturation:** The double-stranded DNA template is heated (≈ 94°C) to separate (denature) the two strands by breaking hydrogen bonds.
2. **Annealing:** The temperature is lowered (≈ 50–65°C) to allow specific short oligonucleotide **primers** to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on each single-stranded template.
3. **Extension:** The temperature is raised (≈ 72°C) and **Taq DNA polymerase** (heat-stable polymerase) extends the primers by adding dNTPs complementary to the template, synthesizing new DNA strands.

These cycles are repeated 30–40 times, exponentially amplifying the target DNA sequence.

### Q12.
Mention any two polluting effects of improper sewage disposal in water bodies.

**Answer:**
1. **Oxygen depletion and eutrophication:** Sewage introduces large amounts of organic matter and nutrients (nitrates, phosphates) into water bodies, promoting excessive algal growth (algal blooms). Decomposition of this organic matter by bacteria depletes dissolved oxygen (BOD increases), leading to the death of fish and other aquatic organisms.
2. **Spread of infectious diseases:** Improperly disposed sewage contains pathogenic bacteria, viruses, and parasites (e.g., *Vibrio cholerae*, *Salmonella typhi*) that contaminate drinking water sources and spread water-borne diseases like cholera and typhoid.

### Q13.
What is mutualism? Provide one suitable example.

**Answer:**
**Mutualism** is a type of symbiotic interaction between two species in which **both species benefit** from the association. Neither species is harmed; instead, both gain fitness advantages.

**Example:** **Mycorrhiza** — a mutualistic association between fungi and plant roots. The fungus helps the plant absorb water and minerals (especially phosphorus) from the soil, while the plant provides organic carbon (sugars) to the fungus. Both organisms benefit from this relationship.

### Q14.
List any two salient features of C4 plants regarding photosynthesis.

**Answer:**
1. **Kranz anatomy:** C4 plants possess a characteristic leaf anatomy called Kranz anatomy, in which the vascular bundles are surrounded by a layer of bundle sheath cells (containing large chloroplasts without grana) and an outer layer of mesophyll cells. CO₂ fixation occurs in mesophyll cells and the Calvin cycle occurs in bundle sheath cells.
2. **Initial CO₂ fixation into a 4-carbon compound:** In C4 plants, CO₂ is initially fixed by the enzyme **PEP carboxylase (PEPcase)** in mesophyll cells to form a 4-carbon compound (oxaloacetate/malate), which is then transported to bundle sheath cells where CO₂ is released and fixed again by Rubisco for the Calvin cycle. This mechanism reduces photorespiration significantly.

### Q16.
How is *Agrobacterium tumefaciens* used in plant genetic engineering?

**Answer:**
*Agrobacterium tumefaciens* is a soil bacterium that naturally infects plants and transfers a segment of its plasmid DNA called **Ti plasmid (tumor-inducing plasmid)**, specifically the **T-DNA (transfer DNA)**, into the plant genome.

In genetic engineering:
- The disease-causing genes in the Ti plasmid are removed and replaced with the desired **gene of interest**.
- This disarmed *Agrobacterium* is then used to infect plant cells (e.g., leaf disc explants).
- The T-DNA carrying the desired gene integrates into the plant's nuclear genome, resulting in a **transgenic plant** expressing the new trait (e.g., pest resistance, herbicide tolerance).

### Q18.
Explain any two adaptations in xerophytes.

**Answer:**
Xerophytes are plants adapted to survive in arid (dry) environments with minimal water availability. Two key adaptations are:

1. **Thick cuticle:** Xerophytes possess a thick, waxy cuticle on their leaf surfaces that is nearly impermeable to water, significantly reducing cuticular transpiration and water loss to the atmosphere.
2. **Sunken stomata (Crypts):** The stomata in xerophytes are sunken into pits or grooves in the leaf epidermis. This creates a humid microenvironment around the stomatal opening, reducing the water vapour gradient and thereby minimizing transpirational water loss.

### Q19.
What is the importance of ecological pyramids?

**Answer:**
Ecological pyramids are graphical representations of the trophic structure of an ecosystem, showing the relationship between different trophic levels. Their importance includes:

1. **Representation of energy flow:** They illustrate the transfer of energy from producers to consumers across trophic levels, showing that energy decreases at each successive level (approximately 10% is transferred — 10% law).
2. **Assessment of ecosystem health:** Ecological pyramids help ecologists analyze the structure, productivity, and health of an ecosystem. An inverted pyramid (e.g., pyramid of biomass in aquatic ecosystems) can indicate unusual ecological dynamics or imbalances.
3. They help in understanding the **feeding relationships** and relative abundance of organisms at different trophic levels.

### Q1.
Describe the structure of a mature ovule in angiosperms.

**Answer:**
A mature ovule (megasporangium) in angiosperms consists of the following parts:

1. **Funicle:** The stalk that attaches the ovule to the placenta of the ovary wall.
2. **Hilum:** The point of attachment of the funicle to the body of the ovule.
3. **Integuments:** One or two protective layers surrounding the nucellus (outer integument and inner integument in most angiosperms).
4. **Micropyle:** A small opening/pore at one end of the ovule formed by the integuments, through which the pollen tube enters during fertilization.
5. **Chalaza:** The basal region of the ovule opposite the micropyle, where integuments and nucellus merge.
6. **Nucellus:** The central mass of parenchymatous tissue enclosed by integuments, which provides nutrition to the developing embryo sac.
7. **Embryo sac (Female gametophyte):** Located within the nucellus; a mature embryo sac contains **7 cells and 8 nuclei**:
   - **1 egg cell** (at the micropylar end)
   - **2 synergids** (flanking the egg cell, forming the egg apparatus)
   - **3 antipodal cells** (at the chalazal end)
   - **1 central cell** containing **2 polar nuclei**

### Q2.
Explain the steps of DNA fingerprinting and its significance in forensic science.

**Answer:**
**Steps of DNA Fingerprinting (developed by Alec Jeffreys):**

1. **DNA extraction:** DNA is isolated from the biological sample (blood, saliva, hair, semen, or any nucleated cell).
2. **Restriction digestion:** The extracted DNA is cut into fragments of varying sizes using specific restriction endonucleases.
3. **Gel electrophoresis:** The DNA fragments are separated by size on an agarose gel under an electric field — smaller fragments travel farther.
4. **Southern blotting:** The separated DNA fragments are transferred (blotted) from the gel onto a nitrocellulose or nylon membrane.
5. **Probe hybridization:** Radioactively labelled (or fluorescent) single-stranded DNA probes complementary to specific VNTRs (Variable Number of Tandem Repeats) or STRs (Short Tandem Repeats) are added; they hybridize to their complementary sequences on the membrane.
6. **Autoradiography:** X-ray film is exposed to the membrane; the labelled probes produce a pattern of bands (the DNA fingerprint) that is unique to each individual.

**Significance in Forensic Science:**
- Used in **criminal investigations** to match DNA from crime scene samples (hair, blood, semen) with suspects.
- Used in **paternity/maternity testing** to establish biological relationships.
- Helps in **identifying missing persons** or victims of disasters by matching DNA with relatives.
- Serves as evidence in **court proceedings** due to its high accuracy and specificity.

### Q4.
How does HIV infect human immune system? Discuss the life cycle briefly.

**Answer:**
HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus that primarily targets and destroys **CD4+ T-helper lymphocytes**, which are central to coordinating the immune response.

**Life Cycle of HIV:**

1. **Attachment:** HIV binds to the **CD4 receptor** (and co-receptors CCR5/CXCR4) on the surface of T-helper cells (and macrophages/dendritic cells) via its envelope glycoprotein (gp120).
2. **Fusion and entry:** The viral envelope fuses with the host cell membrane, releasing the viral contents (RNA genome + enzymes) into the cell cytoplasm.
3. **Reverse transcription:** HIV's **reverse transcriptase** enzyme converts the single-stranded viral RNA genome into double-stranded **DNA** (cDNA) — this is why HIV is called a retrovirus.
4. **Integration:** The viral DNA (provirus) is transported into the nucleus where **integrase** enzyme integrates it into the host cell's chromosomal DNA. The virus can remain latent here for years.
5. **Transcription and translation:** When activated, host RNA polymerase transcribes the proviral DNA into viral RNA, which is translated into viral proteins.
6. **Assembly and budding:** New viral particles are assembled at the cell surface and bud off, acquiring their lipid envelope from the host cell membrane. **Protease** enzyme matures the new virions.
7. **Host cell lysis:** The continuous replication and budding eventually leads to lysis (destruction) of T-helper cells.

**Consequence:** Progressive destruction of CD4+ T cells weakens the immune system, eventually leading to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), where the patient becomes susceptible to opportunistic infections.

### Q5.
List and explain three post-fertilization events in flowering plants.

**Answer:**
Post-fertilization events are changes that occur in the flower after double fertilization:

1. **Endosperm formation:**
   - The **primary endosperm nucleus** (formed by fusion of the two polar nuclei with one sperm nucleus — triple fusion) undergoes repeated mitotic divisions to form the **endosperm**.
   - The endosperm is triploid (3n) and serves as a **nutritive tissue**, supplying nutrients to the developing embryo.
   - It may be nuclear, cellular, or helobial in type.

2. **Embryo development (Embryogenesis):**
   - The **zygote** (formed by the fusion of the egg cell and one sperm nucleus) undergoes mitotic divisions and differentiation to develop into the **embryo**.
   - The process follows a defined pattern: zygote → proembryo → globular embryo → heart-shaped embryo → mature embryo (with cotyledons, plumule, radicle, and hypocotyl).
   - The embryo is the future plant.

3. **Seed and fruit formation:**
   - The **ovule** matures into a **seed** — the integuments form the seed coat (testa), the nucellus may persist as perisperm, and the embryo + endosperm are enclosed.
   - The **ovary wall** (pericarp) develops into the **fruit**, which encloses and protects the seed(s) and aids in dispersal.
   - Other floral parts (petals, sepals, stamens) generally wither and fall off.

### Q6.
Write a short note on bioremediation with an example.

**Answer:**
**Bioremediation** is the use of living organisms — primarily microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, algae) but also plants — to degrade, detoxify, or remove pollutants and contaminants from a polluted environment (soil, water, air), restoring it to its natural state.

**Types:**
- **In-situ bioremediation:** Treatment of contaminated material at the site without excavation.
- **Ex-situ bioremediation:** Excavation of contaminated material followed by treatment elsewhere.

**Mechanisms involved:**
- Biodegradation (breaking down organic pollutants)
- Bioaccumulation (accumulation of pollutants within organisms)
- Biostimulation (stimulating native microbial activity by adding nutrients)

**Example:**
- ***Pseudomonas putida*** (and other hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria) can metabolize and break down **crude oil components** into harmless byproducts like CO₂ and water. These bacteria are used in **oil spill cleanup** operations (e.g., treatment of beaches affected by oil tanker accidents).
- **Phytoremediation** using *Brassica juncea* or sunflowers to absorb heavy metals (like lead, arsenic) from contaminated soil is another example.

### Q7.
Explain the mechanism of photoperiodism in flowering plants.

**Answer:**
**Photoperiodism** is the physiological response of plants to the relative duration of light and darkness (photoperiod) in a 24-hour cycle, particularly the induction of flowering.

**Classification of plants based on photoperiodism:**
1. **Short-day plants (SDPs):** Flower when the length of continuous darkness exceeds a critical minimum (e.g., *Chrysanthemum*, *Tobacco*, *Rice*). (Misnomer — they actually require a critical dark period.)
2. **Long-day plants (LDPs):** Flower when the light period exceeds a critical minimum (or dark period is shorter than critical) (e.g., *Wheat*, *Spinach*, *Radish*).
3. **Day-neutral plants:** Flowering is not controlled by photoperiod (e.g., *Tomato*, *Sunflower*, *Cucumber*).

**Role of phytochrome:**
- The photoreceptor **phytochrome** (a pigment-protein complex) perceives light signals in the **leaves**.
- Phytochrome exists in two interconvertible forms:
  - **Pr (phytochrome red):** absorbs red light (660 nm) → converts to Pfr
  - **Pfr (phytochrome far-red):** absorbs far-red light (730 nm) → converts to Pr; Pfr is the active, biologically effective form
- In darkness, Pfr slowly reverts to Pr. The ratio of Pr:Pfr and the duration of dark period determines whether a plant will flower.

**Florigen hypothesis:**
- Upon receiving the appropriate photoperiod signal, leaves produce a transmissible flowering stimulus called **florigen** (now identified as the protein product of the *FT* gene), which is transported to the shoot apex to initiate flowering.

Enumerate three advantages of using genetically modified (GM) crops.

### Q9.
With a suitable example, describe the process of gene therapy.

**Answer:**
**Gene therapy** is a technique used to correct or replace defective/mutated genes in an individual's cells to treat or prevent genetic diseases. It involves the introduction of a functional, therapeutic gene into target cells to compensate for a non-functional or absent gene.

**Types:**
- **Somatic gene therapy:** Genes are introduced into somatic (body) cells — changes are not heritable.
- **Germline gene therapy:** Genes are introduced into germ cells (eggs/sperm) — changes are heritable (ethically controversial).

**Example: Gene therapy for SCID (Severe Combined Immunodeficiency)**
- SCID is caused by a deficiency of the enzyme **ADA (Adenosine Deaminase)** due to mutations in the *ADA* gene. Without ADA, toxic metabolites accumulate and destroy T and B lymphocytes, severely impairing immunity.

**Steps:**
1. **Isolation:** Lymphocytes (or hematopoietic stem cells) are isolated from the patient's blood/bone marrow.
2. **Ex vivo modification:** A functional copy of the **ADA gene** is introduced into the isolated cells using a **retroviral vector** (or other viral vectors) as the delivery vehicle.
3. **Selection and culture:** Successfully transduced cells (now carrying the functional *ADA* gene) are selected and cultured in vitro.
4. **Reinfusion:** The genetically corrected cells are reinfused/transplanted back into the patient's body.
5. **Outcome:** The corrected cells produce functional ADA enzyme, restoring immune function and alleviating the disease.

This was the **first clinical gene therapy trial** conducted in 1990 by French Anderson on a 4-year-old girl with ADA-SCID.

### Q10.
Discuss the role of decomposers in ecosystem functioning.

**Answer:**
**Decomposers** (also called saprotrophs or reducers) are organisms — primarily bacteria and fungi — that break down dead organic matter (detritus) from dead plants, animals, and their waste products.

**Role of decomposers in ecosystem functioning:**

1. **Breakdown of organic matter (Decomposition):**
   - Decomposers secrete **extracellular enzymes** (proteases, cellulases, lipases) that break down complex organic molecules (proteins, carbohydrates, fats) in dead organic matter into simpler inorganic substances through the processes of:
     - **Fragmentation** (breaking into smaller pieces by detritivores)
     - **Leaching** (water-soluble nutrients pass into the soil)
     - **Catabolism/humification** (formation of humus — dark, amorphous substance)
     - **Mineralisation** (release of inorganic minerals from humus)

2. **Nutrient cycling:**
   - By mineralizing organic matter, decomposers **release inorganic nutrients** (N, P, K, S, etc.) back into the soil and atmosphere, making them available again for producers (plants). This is essential for **biogeochemical cycles** (nitrogen cycle, carbon cycle, phosphorus cycle).

3. **Maintenance of soil fertility:**
   - Decomposers produce **humus**, which improves soil structure, water retention capacity, and fertility, supporting plant growth.

4. **Energy flow:**
   - Decomposers occupy the role of **detritivores/reducers** in food webs, channeling energy from dead organic matter (the detritus food chain) back into the ecosystem. They ensure that energy locked in dead biomass is not permanently lost from the ecosystem.

5. **Waste management:**
   - Decomposers act as natural waste managers, preventing the accumulation of dead organic matter and maintaining cleanliness in ecosystems.

Without decomposers, nutrients would remain locked in dead organic matter, soil fertility would decline, and life on Earth would be unsustainable.

## Section D — Case Study / Source-Based Questions

### Case Study:
A researcher studies two different lakes. Lake A has clear water and a diverse range of fish, algae, and aquatic plants. Lake B, on the other hand, experiences sudden algal blooms and foul smell, and most fish are dead. Upon investigation, the presence of excessive nitrates and phosphates is found in Lake B.

---

**(a) Identify and define the phenomenon affecting Lake B.**

**Answer:**
The phenomenon affecting Lake B is **Eutrophication**.

**Definition:** Eutrophication is the process by which a water body becomes overly enriched with nutrients (particularly nitrates and phosphates), leading to excessive growth of algae and aquatic plants (algal blooms). The subsequent decomposition of this biomass by bacteria depletes dissolved oxygen in the water, creating **hypoxic (oxygen-deficient)** conditions that kill fish and other aquatic organisms.

- When caused by human activities (agricultural runoff, sewage discharge), it is termed **cultural eutrophication** or **anthropogenic eutrophication**.
- The dense algal mat on the surface blocks sunlight penetration, further disrupting the aquatic ecosystem.

---

**(b) What are the likely sources of the substances leading to this phenomenon?**

**Answer:**
The likely sources of excess nitrates and phosphates (nutrients) leading to eutrophication in Lake B are:

1. **Agricultural runoff:** Excessive use of chemical fertilizers (containing nitrates and phosphates) on nearby farmlands. During rain or irrigation, these nutrients leach into the lake through surface runoff and groundwater.
2. **Untreated sewage discharge:** Domestic and municipal sewage contains high levels of organic matter, nitrogen compounds, and phosphates (from human waste and detergents).
3. **Industrial effluents:** Discharge from food processing industries, dairies, or chemical plants can introduce nutrients.
4. **Detergents:** Phosphate-containing detergents from households and laundry washing can contribute phosphates through drainage.
5. **Animal waste/manure** from nearby livestock farms also contributes nitrogen and phosphorus.

---

**(c) Suggest two possible measures to restore the health of Lake B.**

**Answer:**
1. **Reduction of nutrient input:**
   - **Regulate and reduce fertilizer use** in agricultural areas surrounding the lake; promote organic farming and precision agriculture.
   - **Treat sewage** through proper wastewater treatment plants before discharge; ban/restrict the use of phosphate-containing detergents.
   - Establish **buffer zones or riparian vegetation strips** along the lake banks to absorb and filter nutrient-rich runoff before it reaches the lake.

2. **Active restoration of the lake:**
   - **Aeration:** Introduce artificial aeration systems (e.g., aerators, fountains) to increase dissolved oxygen levels in the water, helping aerobic decomposition and restoring aquatic life.
   - **Bioremediation:** Introduce specific microorganisms or aquatic plants (e.g., *Hydrilla*, water hyacinth in controlled amounts) that absorb excess nutrients.
   - **Dredging:** Remove nutrient-rich sediments from the lake bottom.
   - **Alum treatment (chemical precipitation):** Apply alum (aluminum sulfate) to precipitate and remove phosphates from the water.

### Q1.
Describe the lac operon model in prokaryotes. Explain its regulation in the presence and absence of lactose. Illustrate the significance of such gene regulation mechanisms in cells.

**Answer:**

**The Lac Operon Model** (proposed by Jacob and Monod, 1961)

The *lac* operon (*lac*tose operon) is a classic example of an **inducible operon** in *Escherichia coli* (*E. coli*). It controls the metabolism of lactose.

**Components of the lac operon:**

1. **Regulatory gene (*i* gene):** Located upstream; codes for the **lac repressor protein**.
2. **Promoter (P):** The site where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription.
3. **Operator (O):** The DNA sequence to which the repressor binds; located between the promoter and structural genes.
4. **Structural genes:**
   - ***lacZ*:** Codes for **β-galactosidase** — hydrolyzes lactose into glucose and galactose.
   - ***lacY*:** Codes for **permease** — facilitates transport of lactose into the cell.
   - ***lacA*:** Codes for **transacetylase** — transfers acetyl group (role less clear).

[Diagram: Linear representation of the lac operon showing: Regulatory gene (*i*) → Promoter (P) → Operator (O) → *lacZ* → *lacY* → *lacA* → Terminator. Repressor protein produced by *i* gene shown binding to operator.]

---

**Regulation in the ABSENCE of lactose (Operon OFF):**

1. The *i* gene is continuously transcribed and translated to produce the **lac repressor protein**.
2. The active repressor binds with high affinity to the **operator (O)** region.
3. The repressor physically **blocks RNA polymerase** from transcribing the structural genes (*lacZ, lacY, lacA*).
4. No mRNA is produced → No enzymes are synthesized → Lactose is not metabolized.
5. This is the **default/OFF state** when lactose is absent (and glucose is present as the preferred carbon source).

---

**Regulation in the PRESENCE of lactose (Operon ON):**

1. When lactose is present in the medium, it is transported into the cell and a small amount is converted to **allolactose** (the true inducer) by a few pre-existing β-galactosidase molecules.
2. Allolactose (the **inducer**) binds to the **lac repressor protein**, causing a conformational change.
3. The allolactose-repressor complex has **low affinity** for the operator and dissociates from it.
4. With the operator free, **RNA polymerase** can bind to the promoter and transcribe the structural genes.
5. The resulting mRNA is translated into **β-galactosidase, permease, and transacetylase**.
6. These enzymes metabolize lactose efficiently.
7. When lactose is exhausted, allolactose levels fall → repressor reassumes active conformation → binds operator again → operon turns OFF.

$$\text{Lactose} \xrightarrow{\text{(small amount)}} \text{Allolactose (inducer)} + \text{Repressor} \rightarrow \text{Inactive complex} \rightarrow \text{Operator free} \rightarrow \text{Transcription ON}$$

*(Note: Catabolite repression/CAP-cAMP positive regulation also plays a role: when glucose is absent, cAMP levels rise, cAMP binds CAP protein, and the CAP-cAMP complex binds to the promoter region to enhance transcription. This ensures maximal induction only when lactose is present AND glucose is absent.)*

---

**Significance of Gene Regulation Mechanisms:**

1. **Energy conservation:** By expressing genes only when their products are needed (and not continuously), cells conserve significant amounts of energy and biosynthetic resources. Expressing *lacZ* only when lactose is present prevents wasteful enzyme production.

2. **Adaptation to environment:** Gene regulation allows organisms to rapidly adapt their metabolism to changing environmental conditions (e.g., switching between different carbon sources based on availability).

3. **Cellular differentiation:** In multicellular eukaryotes, differential gene expression is the basis of cell differentiation — all cells have the same genome, but different genes are expressed in different cell types (e.g., hemoglobin genes in RBCs, insulin gene in pancreatic β-cells).

4. **Development and growth:** Regulated expression of developmental genes controls the orderly development of organisms from a single fertilized egg.

5. **Disease prevention:** Dysregulation of gene expression (especially in oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes) leads to diseases like cancer, underlining the critical importance of proper regulatory mechanisms.

### Q2.
Discuss the various evidence supporting the theory of evolution and mention the contribution of molecular biology to evolutionary studies.

**Answer:**

**Evidence Supporting the Theory of Evolution:**

**1. Fossil Record (Paleontological Evidence):**
- Fossils are preserved remains or impressions of organisms that lived in the past.
- The fossil record shows a **progression from simpler to more complex organisms** over geological time.
- **Transitional fossils** (e.g., *Archaeopteryx* — showing features of both reptiles and birds) provide evidence of evolutionary transitions between major groups.
- Radioactive dating (carbon dating, potassium-argon dating) allows accurate determination of the age of fossils.
- Gaps in the fossil record ("missing links") continue to be filled by new discoveries.

**2. Comparative Anatomy:**
- **Homologous organs:** Organs that have a similar basic structure and embryological origin but may perform different functions in different species (e.g., forelimbs of humans, horses, whales, and bats). This indicates **divergent evolution** from a common ancestor.
- **Analogous organs:** Organs that perform similar functions but have different structural origins (e.g., wings of birds and wings of insects). This indicates **convergent evolution** — different lineages independently evolving similar adaptations.
- **Vestigial organs:** Reduced, functionless, or rudimentary structures that were functional in ancestors (e.g., human appendix, coccyx, ear muscles). These are remnants of evolutionary history.

**3. Embryological Evidence:**
- **Biogenetic law** (Ernst Haeckel): "Ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny" — embryos of different vertebrates show striking similarities in early developmental stages (e.g., all vertebrate embryos possess pharyngeal pouches and a tail at some stage), suggesting common ancestry.
- Embryos of fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals look remarkably similar in early stages, diverging as development proceeds.

**4. Biogeographical Evidence:**
- The **geographical distribution** of species supports evolution. Species on isolated islands often resemble those on the nearest mainland, suggesting colonization and subsequent evolution (e.g., Darwin's finches in the Galápagos Islands — 14 species evolved from a common ancestor, showing **adaptive radiation**).
- Continental drift explains the distribution of related species on different continents.

**5. Direct Observation of Evolution:**
- **Natural selection** can be directly observed, e.g., development of **antibiotic resistance in bacteria** (rapid evolution), **industrial melanism** in *Biston betularia* (peppered moth), and **pesticide resistance** in insects.

---

**Contribution of Molecular Biology to Evolutionary Studies:**

1. **DNA sequence comparisons:**
   - Comparison of DNA/gene sequences between species reveals the degree of genetic relatedness. Closely related species share more DNA sequence similarity.
   - **Cytochrome c gene** sequences, for example, are nearly identical between humans and chimpanzees (~99% similarity), confirming their close evolutionary relationship.

2. **Protein sequence analysis:**
   - Comparison of amino acid sequences of conserved proteins (e.g., cytochrome c, hemoglobin) across species gives insights into evolutionary divergence — the more similar the sequences, the more recently the species shared a common ancestor.

3. **Molecular phylogenetics:**
   - Using DNA sequences to construct **phylogenetic trees** (evolutionary trees) that depict the evolutionary relationships and divergence times among species with greater accuracy than morphological data alone.

4. **Molecular clock:**
   - The concept that DNA sequences accumulate mutations at a roughly constant rate over time, allowing scientists to estimate **divergence times** between species based on genetic differences.

5. **Horizontal gene transfer:**
   - Molecular biology revealed that genes can be transferred between distantly related organisms (especially in prokaryotes), complicating simple tree-like evolutionary models and supporting the concept of a "web of life."

6. **Discovery of pseudogenes and non-coding DNA:**
   - The presence of **pseudogenes** (non-functional copies of genes) and shared repetitive DNA sequences in related species provides molecular evidence of common ancestry.

7. **Genomic comparisons (Comparative genomics):**
   - Whole-genome sequencing and comparison (e.g., human and chimpanzee genomes are ~98.7% identical) provides the most comprehensive molecular evidence for evolution.

### Q3.
Explain the steps involved in the production of human insulin using recombinant DNA technology.

**Answer:**

Insulin is a protein hormone essential for regulating blood glucose. Diabetic patients require insulin injections. Before recombinant DNA technology, insulin was extracted from pig/cattle pancreas (animal insulin), which caused allergic reactions. In 1982, **Eli Lilly** produced the first recombinant human insulin (**Humulin**) using *E. coli*.

**Structure of Human Insulin:**
- Human insulin consists of two polypeptide chains:
  - **Chain A:** 21 amino acids
  - **Chain B:** 30 amino acids
- The two chains are connected by **disulfide bonds**.
- Insulin is initially synthesized as a single-chain precursor called **proinsulin**, which contains an additional connecting **C-peptide** that is removed during maturation.

---

**Steps in Production of Human Insulin:**

**Step 1 — Isolation/synthesis of insulin genes:**
- The genes encoding **Chain A** and **Chain B** of human insulin are **chemically synthesized** (using gene synthesis/oligonucleotide synthesis) in the laboratory, as the natural gene contains introns that bacteria cannot process.
- Alternatively, cDNA can be synthesized from mRNA of insulin-producing pancreatic β-cells using reverse transcriptase.

**Step 2 — Insertion into cloning vector:**
- Each synthesized gene (Chain A and Chain B) is separately inserted into a **plasmid vector** (e.g., pBR322) downstream of the **β-galactosidase (*lacZ*)** gene promoter, so that insulin chains are expressed as **fusion proteins** with β-galactosidase.
- **Restriction enzymes** are used to cut both the vector and the insulin gene at compatible sites, and **DNA ligase** joins them to create recombinant plasmids.

**Step 3 — Transformation into host bacteria (*E. coli*):**
- The recombinant plasmids (one carrying Chain A gene, another carrying Chain B gene) are introduced into *E. coli* cells separately by **transformation** (using heat shock or electroporation).
- *E. coli* cells that have taken up the recombinant plasmid are selected using **antibiotic resistance markers** on the plasmid.

**Step 4 — Expression and production:**
- The transformed *E. coli* cells are cultured in large fermenters (bioreactors) under optimal conditions.
- The bacteria express the recombinant genes and produce large quantities of **insulin A chain** and **insulin B chain** separately as fusion proteins with β-galactosidase.

**Step 5 — Extraction and purification:**
- The bacterial cells are lysed and the **fusion proteins** are extracted.
- The insulin chains are cleaved from β-galactosidase using **cyanogen bromide (CNBr)** chemical treatment.
- The individual A and B chains are purified by chromatography.

**Step 6 — Formation of functional insulin:**
- The purified **Chain A** and **Chain B** are combined under controlled conditions.
- **Disulfide bonds** form between the chains (and within Chain A) to produce properly folded, biologically active **human insulin**.

**Step 7 — Quality control and packaging:**
- The recombinant human insulin is tested for purity, potency, and safety before being formulated into pharmaceutical preparations for clinical use.

---

**Significance:**
- Recombinant human insulin is identical to natural human insulin, eliminating allergic reactions caused by animal insulin.
- It can be produced in large quantities at lower cost.
- This was a landmark achievement in biotechnology, paving the way for production of many other therapeutic proteins.

### Q4.
Describe in detail how population growth can be represented mathematically. Discuss logistic and exponential growth models and their relevance in ecology.

**Answer:**

Population ecology studies the size, density, and dynamics of populations and the factors that affect them.

**Key terms:**
- **Population size (N):** Total number of individuals in a population.
- **Birth rate (b/natality):** Number of births per individual per unit time.
- **Death rate (d/mortality):** Number of deaths per individual per unit time.
- **Intrinsic rate of natural increase (r):** $r = b - d$ (the per capita rate of population growth under ideal conditions).
- **Carrying capacity (K):** The maximum population size that an environment can sustainably support given available resources.

---

**1. Exponential Growth Model (J-shaped growth)**

When resources are **unlimited** (food, space, mates), a population grows at its maximum intrinsic rate *r*. This is described by the **exponential growth equation:**

**Differential form:**
$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN$$

**Integral form:**
$$N_t = N_0 \cdot e^{rt}$$

Where:
- $N_t$ = population size at time $t$
- $N_0$ = initial population size
- $r$ = intrinsic rate of natural increase
- $e$ = base of natural logarithm (≈ 2.718)
- $t$ = time

**Characteristics:**
- Population size increases **without limit** — producing a **J-shaped curve** when plotted against time.
- The rate of growth increases as population size increases.
- This model applies when resources are abundant, e.g., bacteria introduced into a nutrient-rich medium, or populations introduced into a new environment with no predators.
- **Example:** *Paramecium* populations in rich nutrient broth, early human population growth.

**Limitations:** No real population can grow exponentially indefinitely because resources (food, water, space, mates) are finite in nature.

---

**2. Logistic Growth Model (S-shaped / Sigmoidal growth)**

In nature, resources are **limited**. As population density increases, intraspecific competition for resources intensifies, increasing death rates and decreasing birth rates. This leads to **density-dependent regulation**. The logistic model incorporates the concept of carrying capacity (K):

**Verhulst-Pearl logistic equation:**
$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(\frac{K - N}{K}\right)$$

Where:
- $\frac{K-N}{K}$ = the **unutilized opportunity for growth** (or the "braking factor")
- When $N \ll K$: $\frac{K-N}{K} \approx 1$, so growth is approximately exponential.
- When $N = K/2$: growth rate is maximal (inflection point of the S-curve).
- When $N = K$: $\frac{K-N}{K} = 0$, so $\frac{dN}{dt} = 0$ — population growth stops.
- When $N > K$: growth rate becomes negative — population decreases back toward K.

**Characteristics:**
- Produces an **S-shaped (sigmoid/sigmoidal) curve** when plotted against time.
- Three phases:
  - **Lag phase:** Slow initial growth (small N, organisms acclimatizing).
  - **Exponential (log) phase:** Rapid growth as N increases.
  - **Stationary phase:** Growth slows as N approaches K; births ≈ deaths.
- Population stabilizes around the carrying capacity K.
- **Example:** Sheep population in Tasmania, deer populations in national parks, yeast growth in a flask.

[Diagram: Two graphs side by side. Left: J-shaped exponential growth curve (N vs t, increasing without limit). Right: S-shaped logistic growth curve (N vs t), showing lag phase, exponential phase, and plateau at K (carrying capacity).]

---

**Comparison:**

| Feature | Exponential Growth | Logistic Growth |
|---|---|---|
| Resources | Unlimited | Limited |
| Curve shape | J-shaped | S-shaped (sigmoidal) |
| Growth rate | Increases continuously | Increases then decreases |
| Population limit | No limit | Limited by K |
| Realism | Less realistic | More realistic |
| Equation | $\frac{dN}{dt} = rN$ | $\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(\frac{K-N}{K}\right)$ |

---

**Relevance in Ecology:**

1. **Wildlife management:** Logistic model helps determine **maximum sustainable yield (MSY)** — harvesting at $N = K/2$ where growth rate is maximum, ensuring sustainable exploitation of fish, timber, and wildlife.

2. **Conservation biology:** Understanding growth models helps predict whether small populations of endangered species can recover (if N is well below K, logistic model predicts recovery if r > 0).

3. **Pest control:** Exponential growth models help predict the rapid increase of pest populations; logistic model helps determine when control measures should be applied.

4. **Human population:** While human population growth was historically near-exponential (due to reduced death rates from medicine), logistic considerations (food, water, energy resources) suggest we are approaching carrying capacity, informing policies on resource management and family planning.

5. **Invasive species management:** New species introduced without predators may initially show exponential growth, causing ecological damage before stabilizing or crashing.

### Q5.
What are the consequences of deforestation on biodiversity? Suggest and explain five strategies to manage and conserve forests.

**Answer:**

**Consequences of Deforestation on Biodiversity:**

Deforestation — the large-scale removal of forest cover for agriculture, urbanization, logging, mining, or other human activities — has severe and far-reaching consequences for biodiversity:

1. **Habitat loss and fragmentation:** Forests provide habitat for an estimated 50–80% of terrestrial species. Deforestation destroys these habitats, leaving species with no place to live, feed, or reproduce. Habitat fragmentation isolates populations, reducing gene flow and increasing inbreeding.

2. **Species extinction:** Loss of habitat leads directly to population decline and ultimately **extinction** of species, particularly endemic species with restricted ranges. The IUCN estimates that habitat loss is the primary driver of the current sixth mass extinction event.

3. **Disruption of ecosystem services:** Forests regulate climate, water cycles, soil formation, and nutrient cycling. Deforestation disrupts these services, leading to soil erosion, altered rainfall patterns, increased flooding, and loss of ecosystem productivity.

4. **Climate change:** Forests act as **carbon sinks**. Deforestation releases stored carbon as CO₂, contributing to global warming and climate change, which further threatens biodiversity through altered habitats, extreme weather events, and shifting climatic zones.

5. **Loss of genetic diversity:** With population decline and extinction, the genetic diversity within species is lost permanently, reducing the adaptive potential of surviving populations.

6. **Disruption of food webs:** Loss of keystone species or large numbers of species disrupts predator-prey relationships and entire food webs, causing cascading ecological effects.

7. **Loss of indigenous knowledge:** Deforestation often displaces indigenous communities whose traditional knowledge about medicinal plants and biodiversity is also lost.

---

**Five Strategies to Manage and Conserve Forests:**

**1. Afforestation and Reforestation:**
- **Afforestation** involves planting trees on land that was not previously forested; **reforestation** involves replanting trees in deforested areas.
- Planting native species restores habitat, prevents soil erosion, sequesters carbon, and gradually rebuilds biodiversity.
- Programs like the **Green India Mission** and international initiatives like the Bonn Challenge aim to restore millions of hectares of degraded land.
- *Challenge:* Monoculture plantations offer less biodiversity value than natural forests; restoration should prioritize diverse native species.

**2. Sustainable Forest Management (Sustainable Logging):**
- Regulating timber extraction through **selective logging** (harvesting only mature trees while leaving younger trees and the forest structure intact), **reduced-impact logging**, and **certified sustainable forestry** (e.g., FSC certification).
- Establishing **rotation periods** that allow forests to regenerate before re-harvesting.
- This approach balances economic needs with ecological sustainability, ensuring forests continue to provide services without being permanently destroyed.

**3. Creating Protected Areas (National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, Biosphere Reserves):**
- Designating specific forest areas as **legally protected zones** where logging, mining, hunting, and destructive activities are prohibited or strictly regulated.
- Protected areas provide refuges for endangered species and maintain intact ecosystems.
- **Biosphere reserves** (e.g., Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve in India) include core zones (strict protection), buffer zones (limited use), and transition zones (sustainable use), balancing conservation with local community needs.
- Establishing **wildlife corridors** between protected areas allows species to migrate and maintains gene flow between fragmented populations.

**4. Community-Based Forest Management (Community Forestry / Joint Forest Management):**
- Involving **local communities and indigenous peoples** as active stakeholders in forest management decisions, recognizing their traditional knowledge and rights.
- Programs like **Joint Forest Management (JFM)** in India involve local communities in protecting and managing nearby forests in exchange for a share of benefits (non-timber forest products, eco-tourism income).
- Community stewardship is often more effective and sustainable than top-down government management, as local communities have a direct interest in forest health
