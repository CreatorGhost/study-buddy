### Q1.
If $R = \{(x, y) : x + 2y = 8\}$ is a relation on $\mathbb{N}$, write the range of $R$.

**Answer:** $\{1, 2, 3\}$

**Solution:**
Given $R = \{(x, y) : x + 2y = 8\}$ is a relation on $\mathbb{N}$.

$$2y = 8 - x \implies y = 4 - \frac{x}{2}$$

Substituting natural number values of $x$:
- At $x = 2$: $y = 3$
- At $x = 4$: $y = 2$
- At $x = 6$: $y = 1$

$$\therefore \text{Range} = \{1, 2, 3\}$$

### Q2.
If $\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$, $xy < 1$, then write the value of $x + y + xy$.

**Answer:** $1$

**Solution:**
$$\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

$$\implies \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x + y}{1 - xy}\right) = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

$$\implies \frac{x + y}{1 - xy} = \tan\frac{\pi}{4} = 1$$

$$\implies x + y = 1 - xy$$

$$\therefore x + y + xy = 1$$

### Q3.
If $A$ is a square matrix such that $A^2 = A$, then write the value of $7A - (I + A)^3$, where $I$ is an identity matrix.

**Answer:** $-I$

**Solution:**
Given $A^2 = A$.

$$7A - (I + A)^3$$
$$= 7A - \left[(I + A)^2(I + A)\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[(II + AA + 2AI)(I + A)\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[I + A^2 + 2A\right]\left[I + A\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[I + A + 2A\right]\left[I + A\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[I + 3A\right]\left[I + A\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[II + IA + 3AI + 3A^2\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[I + A + 3A + 3A\right]$$
$$= 7A - \left[I + 7A\right]$$
$$= -I$$

### Q7.

**Solution:**

$$= \frac{1}{2}\left[\log 17 - \log 4\right] = \frac{1}{2}\log\frac{17}{4}$$

### Q11.
If the function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be given by $f(x) = x^2 + 2$ and $g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be given by $g(x) = \dfrac{x}{x - 1}$, $x \neq 1$, find $fog$ and $gof$ and hence find $fog(2)$ and $gof(-3)$.

**Solution:**

**Finding $fog$:**
$$fog = f(g(x)) = f\!\left(\frac{x}{x-1}\right) = \left(\frac{x}{x-1}\right)^2 + 2$$

$$= \frac{x^2}{(x-1)^2} + 2 = \frac{x^2 + 2(x-1)^2}{(x-1)^2}$$

$$= \frac{x^2 + 2x^2 - 4x + 2}{(x-1)^2} = \frac{3x^2 - 4x + 2}{(x-1)^2}$$

**Finding $gof$:**
$$gof = g(f(x)) = g(x^2 + 2) = \frac{x^2 + 2}{(x^2 + 2) - 1} = \frac{x^2 + 2}{x^2 + 1} = 1 + \frac{1}{x^2+1}$$

**Computing $fog(2)$:**
$$fog(2) = \frac{3(2)^2 - 4(2) + 2}{(2-1)^2} = \frac{12 - 8 + 2}{1} = 6$$

**Computing $gof(-3)$:**
$$gof(-3) = 1 + \frac{1}{(-3)^2 + 1} = 1 + \frac{1}{10} = \frac{11}{10} = 1\frac{1}{10}$$

### Q12.
Prove that:
$$\tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\sqrt{1+x} - \sqrt{1-x}}{\sqrt{1+x} + \sqrt{1-x}}\right] = \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\cos^{-1} x, \quad \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} \leq x \leq 1$$

**OR**

If $\tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{x-2}{x-4}\right) + \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{x+2}{x+4}\right) = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$, find the value of $x$.

**Solution (Main):**

In LHS, put $x = \cos 2\theta$:

$$\tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\sqrt{1 + \cos 2\theta} - \sqrt{1 - \cos 2\theta}}{\sqrt{1 + \cos 2\theta} + \sqrt{1 - \cos 2\theta}}\right]$$

Using $1 + \cos 2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta$ and $1 - \cos 2\theta = 2\sin^2\theta$:

$$= \tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\sqrt{2\cos^2\theta} - \sqrt{2\sin^2\theta}}{\sqrt{2\cos^2\theta} + \sqrt{2\sin^2\theta}}\right] = \tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\cos\theta - \sin\theta}{\cos\theta + \sin\theta}\right]$$

$$= \tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{1 - \tan\theta}{1 + \tan\theta}\right] = \tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\tan(\pi/4) - \tan\theta}{1 + \tan(\pi/4)\cdot\tan\theta}\right]$$

$$= \tan^{-1}\!\left[\tan\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4} - \theta\right)\right] = \frac{\pi}{4} - \theta$$

Since $x = \cos 2\theta \implies \theta = \dfrac{\cos^{-1} x}{2}$:

$$= \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\cos^{-1} x = \text{RHS} \qquad \textbf{Proved}$$

---

**Solution (OR):**

$$\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{x-2}{x-4}\right) + \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{x+2}{x+4}\right) = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

Using the addition formula $\tan^{-1} A + \tan^{-1} B = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{A+B}{1-AB}\right)$:

$$\tan^{-1}\!\left[\frac{\dfrac{x-2}{x-4} + \dfrac{x+2}{x+4}}{1 - \dfrac{x-2}{x-4} \cdot \dfrac{x+2}{x+4}}\right] = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

$$\implies \frac{(x-2)(x+4) + (x+2)(x-4)}{(x-4)(x+4) - (x-2)(x+2)} = 1$$

**Numerator:**
$$(x-2)(x+4) + (x+2)(x-4) = x^2 + 2x - 8 + x^2 - 2x - 8 = 2x^2 - 16$$

**Denominator:**
$$(x^2 - 16) - (x^2 - 4) = -12$$

$$\therefore \frac{2x^2 - 16}{-12} = 1 \implies 2x^2 - 16 = -12$$

$$\implies 2x^2 = 4 \implies x^2 = 2 \implies x = \pm\sqrt{2}$$

### Q13.
Using properties of determinants, prove that:

$$\begin{vmatrix} x+y & x & x \\ 5x+4y & 4x & 2x \\ 10x+8y & 8x & 3x \end{vmatrix} = x^3$$

**Solution:**

$$\text{LHS} = \begin{vmatrix} x+y & x & x \\ 5x+4y & 4x & 2x \\ 10x+8y & 8x & 3x \end{vmatrix}$$

Splitting the first column:

$$= \begin{vmatrix} x & x & x \\ 5x & 4x & 2x \\ 10x & 8x & 3x \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} y & x & x \\ 4y & 4x & 2x \\ 8y & 8x & 3x \end{vmatrix}$$

$$= x^3\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 5 & 4 & 2 \\ 10 & 8 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + yx^2\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 4 & 4 & 2 \\ 8 & 8 & 3 \end{vmatrix}$$

In the second determinant, columns 1 and 2 are **not** identical, but after applying $C_1 \to C_1 - C_2$ and $C_2 \to C_2 - C_3$ to the **first** determinant:

$$= x^3\begin{vmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 & 3 \end{vmatrix} + yx^2 \times 0$$

(The second determinant has $C_1 = C_2$ in the second determinant after splitting, giving value $0$.)

Expanding the first determinant along $R_1$:

$$= x^3 \cdot 1 \cdot \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{vmatrix} = x^3(5 - 4) = x^3 = \text{RHS} \qquad \textbf{Proved}$$

### Q14.
Find the value of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ at $\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$, if $x = ae^\theta(\sin\theta - \cos\theta)$ and $y = ae^\theta(\sin\theta + \cos\theta)$.

**Solution:**

Using parametric differentiation:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta} \quad \cdots (1)$$

**Computing $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta}$** (using product rule):

$$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = ae^\theta(\cos\theta - \sin\theta) + ae^\theta(\sin\theta + \cos\theta) = 2ae^\theta\cos\theta$$

**Computing $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta}$:**

$$\frac{dx}{d\theta} = ae^\theta(\cos\theta + \sin\theta) + ae^\theta(\sin\theta - \cos\theta) = 2ae^\theta\sin\theta$$

Substituting into $(1)$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2ae^\theta\cos\theta}{2ae^\theta\sin\theta} = \cot\theta$$

At $\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$:
$$\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{\theta = \pi/4} = \cot\frac{\pi}{4} = 1$$

### Q15.
If $y = Pe^{ax} + Qe^{bx}$, show that:
$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - (a+b)\frac{dy}{dx} + ab\,y = 0$$

**Solution:**

Given:
$$y = Pe^{ax} + Qe^{bx} \quad \cdots (1)$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = aPe^{ax} + bQe^{bx} \quad \cdots (2)$$

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = a^2Pe^{ax} + b^2Qe^{bx} \quad \cdots (3)$$

Multiplying $(1)$ by $ab$:
$$aby = abPe^{ax} + abQe^{bx} \quad \cdots (4)$$

Multiplying $(2)$ by $(a + b)$:
$$(a+b)\frac{dy}{dx} = (a+b)\left(aPe^{ax} + bQe^{bx}\right)$$
$$= \left(a^2Pe^{ax} + b^2Qe^{bx}\right) + \left(abPe^{ax} + abQe^{bx}\right) \quad \cdots (5)$$

Now computing $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} - (a+b)\dfrac{dy}{dx} + aby$:

Using $(3)$, $(5)$, and $(4)$:

$$= \left(a^2Pe^{ax} + b^2Qe^{bx}\right) - \left[\left(a^2Pe^{ax} + b^2Qe^{bx}\right) + \left(abPe^{ax} + abQe^{bx}\right)\right] + \left(abPe^{ax} + abQe^{bx}\right)$$

$$= 0 \qquad \textbf{Proved}$$

### Q17.
Evaluate:
$$\int_0^{\pi} \frac{4x\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

**OR**

Evaluate:
$$\int \frac{x + 2}{\sqrt{x^2 + 5x + 6}}\, dx$$

**Solution (Main):**

$$I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{4x\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

Applying the property $\displaystyle\int_0^a f(x)\,dx = \int_0^a f(a - x)\,dx$:

$$I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{4(\pi - x)\sin(\pi - x)}{1 + \cos^2(\pi - x)}\, dx = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{4(\pi - x)\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

Adding the two expressions:
$$2I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{4\pi\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

Applying $\displaystyle\int_0^{2a} f(x)\,dx = 2\int_0^a f(x)\,dx$ when $f(2a - x) = f(x)$:

$$2I = 4\pi \cdot 2 \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

$$I = 4\pi \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{\sin x}{1 + \cos^2 x}\, dx$$

Put $\cos x = t \implies -\sin x\, dx = dt$

When $x = 0$, $t = 1$; when $x = \pi/2$, $t = 0$:

$$I = 4\pi \int_1^0 \frac{-dt}{1 + t^2} = 4\pi \int_0^1 \frac{dt}{1 + t^2}$$

$$= 4\pi\Big[\tan^{-1} t\Big]_0^1 = 4\pi\left[\tan^{-1} 1 - \tan^{-1} 0\right] = 4\pi \cdot \frac{\pi}{4} = \pi^2$$

---

**Solution (OR):**

$$\int \frac{x + 2}{\sqrt{x^2 + 5x + 6}}\, dx$$

Write $x + 2 = \lambda\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2 + 5x + 6) + \mu = \lambda(2x + 5) + \mu$

Comparing coefficients:
- Coefficient of $x$: $1 = 2\lambda \implies \lambda = \dfrac{1}{2}$
- Constant term: $2 = 5\lambda + \mu \implies \mu = 2 - \dfrac{5}{2} = -\dfrac{1}{2}$

$$\therefore \int \frac{x+2}{\sqrt{x^2+5x+6}}\,dx = \int \frac{\frac{1}{2}(2x+5) - \frac{1}{2}}{\sqrt{x^2+5x+6}}\,dx = I_1 - I_2$$

**Computing $I_1$:**

$$I_1 = \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{2x+5}{\sqrt{x^2+5x+6}}\,dx$$

Put $x^2 + 5x + 6 = t \implies (2x+5)\,dx = dt$:

$$I_1 = \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{dt}{\sqrt{t}} = \sqrt{t} + C_1 = \sqrt{x^2+5x+6} + C_1$$

**Computing $I_2$:**

$$I_2 = \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x^2+5x+6}} = \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{\left(x+\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2}}$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\log\left[\left(x + \frac{5}{2}\right) + \sqrt{x^2 + 5x + 6}\right] + C_2$$

**Final Answer:**

$$I = \sqrt{x^2+5x+6} + \frac{1}{2}\log\left[\left(x + \frac{5}{2}\right) + \sqrt{x^2+5x+6}\right] + C$$

### Q18.
Find the particular solution of the differential equation:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 + x + y + xy$$
given that $y = 0$ when $x = 1$.

**Solution:**

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = (1 + x) + y(1 + x) = (1 + y)(1 + x)$$

Separating variables:
$$\frac{dy}{1 + y} = (1 + x)\,dx$$

Integrating both sides:
$$\int \frac{dy}{1 + y} = \int (1 + x)\,dx$$

$$\log|1 + y| = x + \frac{x^2}{2} + C$$

**Applying initial condition** $y = 0$ when $x = 1$:

$$\log|1| = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + C \implies 0 = \frac{3}{2} + C \implies C = -\frac{3}{2}$$

**Particular solution:**

$$\log|1 + y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + x - \frac{3}{2}$$

This can also be written as:
$$1 + y = e^{(x^2 + 2x - 3)/2} \implies y = e^{(x^2+2x-3)/2} - 1$$

### Q19.
Solve the differential equation:
$$(1 + x^2)\frac{dy}{dx} + y = e^{\tan^{-1} x}$$

**Solution:**

Rewriting in standard linear form:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{y}{1 + x^2} = \frac{e^{\tan^{-1} x}}{1 + x^2}$$

Here $P(x) = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$ and $Q(x) = \dfrac{e^{\tan^{-1} x}}{1+x^2}$.

**Integrating Factor:**
$$IF = e^{\int P\,dx} = e^{\int \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx} = e^{\tan^{-1} x}$$

**General Solution:**
$$y \cdot e^{\tan^{-1} x} = \int e^{\tan^{-1} x} \cdot \frac{e^{\tan^{-1} x}}{1+x^2}\,dx + C$$

$$y \cdot e^{\tan^{-1} x} = \int \frac{\left(e^{\tan^{-1} x}\right)^2}{1+x^2}\,dx + C \quad \cdots (1)$$

To evaluate the integral, put $e^{\tan^{-1} x} = t$:

$$e^{\tan^{-1} x} \cdot \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = dt$$

$$\therefore \int \frac{\left(e^{\tan^{-1} x}\right)^2}{1+x^2}\,dx = \int t\,dt = \frac{t^2}{2} + C = \frac{\left(e^{\tan^{-1} x}\right)^2}{2} + C$$

Substituting back into $(1)$:

$$\boxed{y \cdot e^{\tan^{-1} x} = \frac{\left(e^{\tan^{-1} x}\right)^2}{2} + C}$$

### Q24.
Show that the altitude of the right circular cone of maximum volume that can be inscribed in a sphere of radius $r$ is $\dfrac{4r}{3}$. Also show that the maximum volume of the cone is $\dfrac{8}{27}$ of the volume of the sphere.

**Solution:**

[Diagram: A right circular cone inscribed inside a sphere of radius $r$. The cone has height $h$, base radius $R$, and the centre of the sphere $C$ is at distance $h - r$ from the base of the cone.]

In $\triangle ABX$, by the Pythagorean theorem (where $AC = h - r$):

$$(h - r)^2 + R^2 = r^2 \implies R^2 = r^2 - (h-r)^2$$

**Volume of cone:**

$$V = \frac{1}{3}\pi R^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi\left[r^2 - (h-r)^2\right]h$$

$$= \frac{\pi}{3}\left[r^2 h - h^3 + 2h^2 r - r^2 h\right] = \frac{\pi}{3}\left[2h^2 r - h^3\right]$$

**For maximum volume**, $\dfrac{dV}{dh} = 0$:

$$\frac{dV}{dh} = \frac{\pi}{3}[4hr - 3h^2] = 0$$

$$\implies 4hr = 3h^2 \implies h = \frac{4r}{3}$$

**Verifying maximum** ($d^2V/dh^2 < 0$):

$$\frac{d^2V}{dh^2} = \frac{\pi}{3}[4r - 6h]$$

At $h = \dfrac{4r}{3}$:
$$\frac{d^2V}{dh^2} = \frac{\pi}{3}\left[4r - 8r\right] = -\frac{4\pi r}{3} < 0 \quad \checkmark$$

So $h = \dfrac{4r}{3}$ gives **maximum volume**.

**Maximum Volume of cone** at $h = \dfrac{4r}{3}$:

$$R^2 = r^2 - \left(\frac{4r}{3} - r\right)^2 = r^2 - \frac{r^2}{9} = \frac{8r^2}{9}$$

$$V_{\max} = \frac{1}{3}\pi \cdot \frac{8r^2}{9} \cdot \frac{4r}{3} = \frac{32\pi r^3}{81} = \frac{8}{27} \cdot \frac{4\pi r^3}{3}$$

$$\boxed{V_{\max} = \frac{8}{27} \times \text{Volume of sphere}} \qquad \textbf{Proved}$$

$$= \frac{1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{3}}{1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{3} + \dfrac{3}{4} \cdot \dfrac{1}{3} + 0.6 \cdot \dfrac{1}{3}}$$
