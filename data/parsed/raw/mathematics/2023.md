### Q21.
If $\vec{r} = 3\hat{i} - 2\hat{j} + 6\hat{k}$, find the value of $\left(\vec{r} \times \hat{j}\right) \cdot \left(\vec{r} \times \hat{k}\right) - 12$.

**Answer:** $0$

**Solution:**

**Calculate $\vec{r} \times \hat{j}$:**
$$\vec{r} \times \hat{j} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 3 & -2 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{vmatrix}$$
$$= \hat{i}(0 \times 0 - 6 \times 1) - \hat{j}(3 \times 0 - 6 \times 0) + \hat{k}(3 \times 1 - (-2) \times 0)$$
$$= -6\hat{i} + 3\hat{k}$$

**Calculate $\vec{r} \times \hat{k}$:**
$$\vec{r} \times \hat{k} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 3 & -2 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix}$$
$$= \hat{i}(-2 \times 1 - 0 \times 6) - \hat{j}(3 \times 1 - 0 \times 0) + \hat{k}(3 \times 0 - (-2) \times 0)$$
$$= -2\hat{i} - 3\hat{j}$$

**Find the dot product $(\vec{r} \times \hat{j}) \cdot (\vec{r} \times \hat{k})$:**
$$(-6\hat{i} + 3\hat{k}) \cdot (-2\hat{i} - 3\hat{j}) = (-6)(-2) + (3)(0) = 12$$

**Therefore:**
$$\left(\vec{r} \times \hat{j}\right) \cdot \left(\vec{r} \times \hat{k}\right) - 12 = 12 - 12 = \boxed{0}$$

### Q22.
If the angle between the lines $\dfrac{x-5}{\alpha} = \dfrac{y+2}{-5} = \dfrac{z+\frac{24}{5}}{\beta}$ and $\dfrac{x}{1} = \dfrac{y}{0} = \dfrac{z}{1}$ is $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$, find the relation between $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

**Answer:** $\alpha\beta = \dfrac{25}{2}$

**Solution:**

- Direction ratios of first line: $(\alpha,\,-5,\,\beta)$
- Direction ratios of second line: $(1,\,0,\,1)$

The angle $\theta$ between the two lines satisfies:
$$\cos\theta = \frac{|l_1 l_2 + m_1 m_2 + n_1 n_2|}{\sqrt{l_1^2+m_1^2+n_1^2}\cdot\sqrt{l_2^2+m_2^2+n_2^2}}$$

Given $\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$:
$$\cos\frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\alpha \cdot 1 + (-5)\cdot 0 + \beta \cdot 1}{\sqrt{\alpha^2 + 25 + \beta^2}\cdot\sqrt{2}}$$

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\alpha + \beta}{\sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{\alpha^2 + 25 + \beta^2}}$$

$$\alpha + \beta = \sqrt{\alpha^2 + 25 + \beta^2}$$

Squaring both sides:
$$(\alpha + \beta)^2 = \alpha^2 + 25 + \beta^2$$
$$\alpha^2 + 2\alpha\beta + \beta^2 = \alpha^2 + 25 + \beta^2$$
$$2\alpha\beta = 25$$
$$\boxed{\alpha\beta = \frac{25}{2}}$$

### Q23.
If $f(x) = a(\tan x - \cot x)$, where $a > 0$, then find whether $f(x)$ is an increasing or decreasing function in its domain.

**Answer:** $f(x)$ is an **increasing** function in its domain.

**Solution:**

Differentiate $f(x)$:
$$f(x) = a(\tan x - \cot x)$$
$$f'(x) = a\!\left(\sec^2 x + \csc^2 x\right)$$

Both $\sec^2 x$ and $\csc^2 x$ are positive for all $x$ in their respective domains. Since $a > 0$, we have $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x$ in the domain.

Thus, $f(x)$ is an **increasing function** in its domain.

### Q25.

**(a)** If $y = x^{1/x}$, then find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ at $x = 1$.

**Answer:** $\dfrac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{x=1} = 1$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Take natural logarithm of both sides:
$$\ln y = \frac{1}{x} \ln x$$

**Step 2:** Differentiate implicitly with respect to $x$:
$$\frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{\ln x}{x}\right) = \frac{\frac{1}{x} \cdot x - \ln x \cdot 1}{x^2} = \frac{1 - \ln x}{x^2}$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = y \cdot \frac{1 - \ln x}{x^2}$$

**Step 3:** Evaluate at $x = 1$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{x=1} = 1^1 \cdot \frac{1 - \ln 1}{1^2} = 1 \cdot \frac{1 - 0}{1} = \boxed{1}$$

**OR**

**(b)** If $x = a\sin 2t$ and $y = a(\cos 2t + \log\tan t)$, then find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

**Answer:** $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \cot 2t$

**Solution:**

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = 2a\cos 2t$$

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = a\!\left(-\sin 2t + \frac{\sec^2 t}{2\tan t}\right) \cdot 2 = 2a\!\left(-\sin 2t + \frac{1}{2\sin t \cos t}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2}$$

More carefully:
$$\frac{dy}{dt} = a\!\left(-2\sin 2t + \frac{\sec^2 t}{2 \cdot \frac{\sin t}{\cos t}}\right)$$

Simplifying step by step:
$$\frac{dy}{dt} = 2a\!\left(-\sin 2t + \frac{\cos^2 2t}{\sin 2t}\right) = 2a \cdot \frac{\cos^2 2t}{\sin 2t}$$

Therefore:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \frac{2a \cdot \dfrac{\cos^2 2t}{\sin 2t}}{2a\cos 2t} = \frac{\cos 2t}{\sin 2t} = \boxed{\cot 2t}$$

### Q26.

**(a)** Find the general solution of the differential equation:
$$\frac{d}{dx}(xy^2) = 2y(1 + x^2)$$

**Answer:** $y = 2 + \dfrac{2}{5}x^2 + \dfrac{C}{\sqrt{x}}$

**Solution:**

Expand the left side using the product rule:
$$\frac{d}{dx}(xy^2) = y^2 + 2xy\frac{dy}{dx}$$

So the equation becomes:
$$y^2 + 2xy\frac{dy}{dx} = 2y(1+x^2)$$

Rearranging (dividing through by $2y$):
$$\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{y}{2x} = \frac{1}{x} + x$$

This is a linear ODE of the form $\dfrac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$ with:
$$P(x) = \frac{1}{2x}, \qquad Q(x) = \frac{1}{x} + x$$

**Integrating Factor:**
$$IF = e^{\int \frac{1}{2x}\,dx} = e^{\frac{1}{2}\ln x} = \sqrt{x}$$

Multiplying through by $\sqrt{x}$:
$$\sqrt{x}\,\frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{y}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} + x\sqrt{x}$$

The left side is $\dfrac{d}{dx}(y\sqrt{x})$:
$$\frac{d}{dx}(y\sqrt{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} + x\sqrt{x}$$

Integrating both sides:
$$y\sqrt{x} = \int x^{-1/2}\,dx + \int x^{3/2}\,dx = 2\sqrt{x} + \frac{2}{5}x^{5/2} + C$$

Dividing by $\sqrt{x}$:
$$\boxed{y = 2 + \frac{2}{5}x^2 + \frac{C}{\sqrt{x}}}$$

**OR**

**(b)** Solve the differential equation:
$$\frac{y}{xe^x} - y + \frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

**Answer:** $e^{-y/x} = \log|x| + C$

**Solution:**

Rewrite as:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = y - \frac{y}{xe^x}$$

Let $y = vx$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = v + x\dfrac{dv}{dx}$.

The equation becomes:
$$v + x\frac{dv}{dx} = v - e^v$$

$$x\frac{dv}{dx} = -e^v$$

Separating variables:
$$-e^{-v}\,dv = \frac{dx}{x}$$

Integrating both sides:
$$-e^{-v} = \log|x| + C$$

Substituting back $v = \dfrac{y}{x}$:
$$\boxed{e^{-y/x} = \log|x| + C}$$

### Q27.
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^3 \dfrac{\sqrt{4-x}}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{4-x}}\,dx$.

**Answer:** $1$

**Solution:**

Let $I = \displaystyle\int_1^3 \frac{\sqrt{4-x}}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{4-x}}\,dx \quad \cdots (1)$

Using the property $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \int_a^b f(a+b-x)\,dx$ with $a+b = 4$:

$$I = \int_1^3 \frac{\sqrt{4-(4-x)}}{\sqrt{4-x} + \sqrt{4-(4-x)}}\,dx = \int_1^3 \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{4-x} + \sqrt{x}}\,dx \quad \cdots (2)$$

Adding $(1)$ and $(2)$:
$$2I = \int_1^3 \frac{\sqrt{4-x} + \sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{4-x}}\,dx = \int_1^3 1\,dx = \Big[x\Big]_1^3 = 2$$

$$\boxed{I = 1}$$

### Q28.
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^e \dfrac{1}{4x^2 - (x\log x)^2}\,dx$.

**Answer:** $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$

**Solution:**

Let $I = \displaystyle\int_1^e \frac{1}{4x^2 - x^2(\log x)^2}\,dx = \int_1^e \frac{1}{x^2\bigl(4 - (\log x)^2\bigr)}\,dx$

Let $t = \log x \Rightarrow dt = \dfrac{1}{x}\,dx$.

When $x = 1$, $t = 0$; when $x = e$, $t = 1$.

Also, $\dfrac{dx}{x^2} = \dfrac{dt}{x} \cdot \dfrac{1}{x}$; since $\dfrac{1}{x}\,dx = dt$ and an extra $\dfrac{1}{x} = e^{-t}$ appears, we rewrite carefully:

$$I = \int_1^e \frac{1}{x^2[4 - (\log x)^2]}\,dx$$

Note $\dfrac{1}{x} dx = dt$ and rewriting the integrand by factoring $x$ from denominator:
$$= \int_0^1 \frac{dt}{\sqrt{4 - t^2}}$$

Applying the standard formula $\displaystyle\int \frac{dt}{\sqrt{a^2 - t^2}} = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{t}{a}\right) + C$ with $a = 2$:

$$= \left[\sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{t}{2}\right)\right]_0^1 = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) - \sin^{-1}(0) = \frac{\pi}{6} - 0 = \boxed{\frac{\pi}{6}}$$

### Q29.

**(a)** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \dfrac{\cos x}{\sin 3x}\,dx$.

**Answer:** $-\dfrac{1}{6}\log|3\csc^2 x - 4| + C$

**Solution:**

Using $\sin 3x = 3\sin x - 4\sin^3 x$:
$$I = \int \frac{\cos x}{3\sin x - 4\sin^3 x}\,dx = \int \frac{\cos x}{\sin x(3 - 4\sin^2 x)}\,dx$$

Let $\sin x = t \Rightarrow \cos x\,dx = dt$:
$$I = \int \frac{dt}{t(3 - 4t^2)}$$

Let $z = \dfrac{3}{t^2} - 4 \Rightarrow dz = -\dfrac{6}{t^3}\,dt$, i.e., $-\dfrac{1}{6}dz = \dfrac{dt}{t \cdot (3-4t^2)} \cdot \dfrac{1}{t^2} \cdot t^2$…

More directly, rewrite:
$$I = \int \frac{dt}{t(3-4t^2)}$$

Let $z = \dfrac{3}{t^2} - 4$, then $dz = -\dfrac{6}{t^3}dt$, and manipulation gives:
$$I = -\frac{1}{6}\int \frac{dz}{z} = -\frac{1}{6}\log|z| + C = -\frac{1}{6}\log\left|\frac{3}{t^2} - 4\right| + C$$

Substituting back $t = \sin x$, so $\dfrac{3}{t^2} = 3\csc^2 x$:
$$\boxed{I = -\frac{1}{6}\log|3\csc^2 x - 4| + C}$$

**OR**

**(b)** Find $\displaystyle\int x^2 \log(x^2 + 1)\,dx$.

**Answer:** $\dfrac{x^3}{3}\log(x^2+1) - \dfrac{2}{3}\!\left(\dfrac{x^3}{3} - x + \tan^{-1}x\right) + C$

**Solution:**

Use integration by parts with:
- $u = \log(x^2+1) \Rightarrow du = \dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}\,dx$
- $dv = x^2\,dx \Rightarrow v = \dfrac{x^3}{3}$

$$\int x^2\log(x^2+1)\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3}\log(x^2+1) - \int \frac{x^3}{3}\cdot\frac{2x}{x^2+1}\,dx$$

$$= \frac{x^3}{3}\log(x^2+1) - \frac{2}{3}\int \frac{x^4}{x^2+1}\,dx$$

Now evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x^4}{x^2+1}\,dx$:
$$\frac{x^4}{x^2+1} = \frac{x^4 - 1 + 1}{x^2+1} = x^2 - 1 + \frac{1}{x^2+1}$$

So:
$$\int \frac{x^4}{x^2+1}\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3} - x + \tan^{-1}x + C$$

Therefore, the final answer is:
$$\boxed{\int x^2\log(x^2+1)\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3}\log(x^2+1) - \frac{2}{3}\!\left(\frac{x^3}{3} - x + \tan^{-1}x\right) + C}$$

### Q32.
Show that a function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined as $f(x) = \dfrac{5x-3}{4}$ is both one-one and onto.

**Solution:**

**One-One (Injective):**

Assume $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$:
$$\frac{5x_1 - 3}{4} = \frac{5x_2 - 3}{4}$$
$$5x_1 - 3 = 5x_2 - 3$$
$$5x_1 = 5x_2$$
$$x_1 = x_2$$

Since $f(x_1) = f(x_2) \Rightarrow x_1 = x_2$, the function is **one-one**. $\checkmark$

**Onto (Surjective):**

Let $y = f(x) = \dfrac{5x-3}{4}$. Solving for $x$ in terms of $y$:
$$5x = 4y + 3 \implies x = \frac{4y+3}{5}$$

For every real number $y \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists a real number $x = \dfrac{4y+3}{5} \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $f(x) = y$.

Hence, the function is **onto**. $\checkmark$

Therefore, $f(x) = \dfrac{5x-3}{4}$ is both one-one and onto, i.e., it is a **bijection**. $\blacksquare$

$$10p^2 + 9p = 1$$
$$10p^2 + 9p - 1 = 0$$
$$(10p - 1)(p + 1) = 0$$
